Introduction to the Respiratory System

The respiratory system is a complex biological system responsible for the exchange of gases between the body and the environment. Its primary function is to supply oxygen to the bloodstream and remove carbon dioxide, a metabolic waste product.

Primary Functions

  • Gas Exchange: Oxygen intake and carbon dioxide removal
  • pH Regulation: Maintains blood pH by regulating CO‚ÇÇ levels
  • Voice Production: Air passing through the larynx enables speech
  • Smell Detection: Olfactory receptors in the nasal cavity
  • Protection: Filters, warms, and humidifies incoming air
  • Immune Defense: Removes pathogens and particles from air

System Components Overview

The respiratory system consists of two main divisions:

Upper Respiratory Tract

Components: Nose, nasal cavity, pharynx, and larynx

Function: Filters, warms, and moistens incoming air; provides initial immune defense

Lower Respiratory Tract

Components: Trachea, bronchi, bronchioles, and lungs (alveoli)

Function: Conducts air to gas exchange surfaces; performs actual gas exchange

Detailed Anatomy of the Respiratory System

1. The Nose and Nasal Cavity

The nose serves as the primary entrance for air into the respiratory system.

External Nose

  • Bridge: Superior portion formed by nasal bones
  • Nostrils (Nares): External openings for air entry
  • Nasal Septum: Divides the nose into left and right cavities

Nasal Cavity Features

  • Conchae (Turbinates): Three shelf-like structures (superior, middle, inferior) that increase surface area
  • Olfactory Epithelium: Located in superior region; contains smell receptors
  • Respiratory Mucosa: Pseudostratified ciliated columnar epithelium with goblet cells
  • Rich Blood Supply: Warms incoming air to body temperature
  • Mucus Production: Traps particles and moistens air

Clinical Note: Nasal Function

The nasal cavity can warm air from -40¬∞F to 98.6¬∞F in less than a second and humidify it to 75-80% humidity. The conchae create turbulent airflow, maximizing contact time with the mucosa.

2. The Pharynx (Throat)

A muscular tube approximately 13 cm long that serves both respiratory and digestive systems.

Three Regions:

  • Nasopharynx:
    • Posterior to nasal cavity
    • Contains pharyngeal tonsil (adenoids)
    • Opening to auditory (Eustachian) tubes
    • Respiratory function only
  • Oropharynx:
    • Posterior to oral cavity
    • Contains palatine and lingual tonsils
    • Passageway for both air and food
  • Laryngopharynx:
    • Extends from hyoid bone to esophagus
    • Diverges into larynx (anterior) and esophagus (posterior)

3. The Larynx (Voice Box)

Located between the pharynx and trachea, the larynx contains the vocal cords and protects the airway during swallowing.

Cartilages of the Larynx:

  • Thyroid Cartilage: Largest; forms Adam's apple
  • Cricoid Cartilage: Ring-shaped; inferior to thyroid cartilage
  • Epiglottis: Elastic cartilage that covers laryngeal inlet during swallowing
  • Arytenoid Cartilages: Anchor and move vocal cords
  • Corniculate and Cuneiform: Small supporting cartilages

Vocal Cords:

  • True Vocal Cords: Vibrate to produce sound
  • False Vocal Cords: Superior to true cords; protect and close during swallowing
  • Glottis: Opening between vocal cords

Voice Production Mechanism

Sound is produced when exhaled air passes through the glottis, causing the vocal cords to vibrate. Pitch is controlled by cord tension (controlled by intrinsic laryngeal muscles), while volume depends on air pressure.

4. The Trachea (Windpipe)

A tube approximately 12 cm long and 2.5 cm in diameter extending from the larynx to the bronchi.

Structure:

  • C-Shaped Cartilage Rings: 16-20 hyaline cartilage rings prevent collapse
  • Trachealis Muscle: Smooth muscle connecting posterior ends of cartilage rings
  • Mucosal Lining: Pseudostratified ciliated columnar epithelium
  • Cilia: Beat upward to move mucus toward pharynx (mucociliary escalator)
  • Goblet Cells: Produce mucus to trap particles

5. The Bronchi and Bronchioles

The trachea divides at the carina (T5 level) into right and left primary bronchi.

Bronchial Tree Hierarchy:

1

Primary (Main) Bronchi

Right: wider, shorter, more vertical (more prone to aspiration). Left: narrower, longer, more horizontal. Both contain C-shaped cartilage rings.

2

Secondary (Lobar) Bronchi

3 in right lung (upper, middle, lower lobes); 2 in left lung (upper, lower lobes). Cartilage becomes irregular plates.

3

Tertiary (Segmental) Bronchi

Supply bronchopulmonary segments (10 in right, 8-10 in left). Further reduction in cartilage.

4

Bronchioles

Diameter < 1 mm. No cartilage; supported by smooth muscle. Epithelium transitions to simple columnar/cuboidal.

5

Terminal Bronchioles

Last conducting airways. Simple cuboidal epithelium with Clara cells (produce surfactant).

6

Respiratory Bronchioles

First site of gas exchange. Occasional alveoli in walls.

7

Alveolar Ducts and Sacs

Completely lined with alveoli. Primary site of gas exchange.

6. The Lungs

Two cone-shaped organs occupying the thoracic cavity, separated by the mediastinum.

General Structure:

  • Apex: Superior tip extending above clavicle
  • Base: Inferior surface resting on diaphragm
  • Hilum: Medial depression where bronchi, vessels, and nerves enter
  • Costal Surface: Anterior, lateral, and posterior surfaces against ribs

Lobes and Fissures:

  • Right Lung: 3 lobes (superior, middle, inferior) separated by horizontal and oblique fissures
  • Left Lung: 2 lobes (superior, inferior) separated by oblique fissure; contains cardiac notch

Pleural Membranes:

  • Visceral Pleura: Adheres directly to lung surface
  • Parietal Pleura: Lines thoracic wall, diaphragm, and mediastinum
  • Pleural Cavity: Potential space containing serous fluid (lubricates, creates surface tension)
  • Intrapleural Pressure: Slightly negative (~-4 mmHg), keeps lungs inflated

7. The Alveoli

Microscopic air sacs where gas exchange occurs. Each lung contains 150-250 million alveoli.

Alveolar Structure:

  • Type I Pneumocytes:
    • 95% of alveolar surface area
    • Extremely thin (0.2-0.5 Œºm) for gas diffusion
    • Connected by tight junctions
  • Type II Pneumocytes:
    • 5% of surface; cuboidal shape
    • Produce pulmonary surfactant (reduces surface tension)
    • Can differentiate into Type I cells for repair
  • Alveolar Macrophages:
    • Phagocytose dust, debris, and pathogens
    • Part of innate immune system

Respiratory Membrane:

The barrier between air and blood consists of:

  1. Alveolar epithelium (Type I pneumocyte)
  2. Fused basement membranes
  3. Capillary endothelium

Total thickness: 0.5 Œºm (extremely thin for rapid diffusion)

Surfactant Importance

Pulmonary surfactant is a complex mixture of phospholipids (mainly dipalmitoylphosphatidylcholine) and proteins. It reduces surface tension in alveoli, preventing collapse during expiration and reducing the work of breathing. Premature infants often lack sufficient surfactant, leading to respiratory distress syndrome.

Respiratory Physiology

Pulmonary Ventilation (Breathing)

Pulmonary ventilation is the physical movement of air into and out of the lungs, driven by pressure gradients.

Pressure Relationships:

  • Atmospheric Pressure (Patm): 760 mmHg at sea level
  • Intrapulmonary Pressure (Ppul): Pressure inside alveoli
  • Intrapleural Pressure (Pip): Pressure in pleural cavity (~756 mmHg at rest)

P‚ÇÅV‚ÇÅ = P‚ÇÇV‚ÇÇ - At constant temperature, pressure and volume are inversely related. When thoracic volume increases, intrapulmonary pressure decreases, causing air to flow in. When volume decreases, pressure increases, forcing air out.

Inspiration (Inhalation)

1

Diaphragm Contracts

Primary respiratory muscle contracts and flattens, increasing vertical thoracic dimension by ~1 cm (quiet breathing) to 10 cm (deep breathing).

2

External Intercostals Contract

Ribs elevate and sternum moves forward/upward, increasing anteroposterior and lateral dimensions.

3

Thoracic Volume Increases

Chest cavity expands, causing intrapleural pressure to become more negative (~-6 mmHg).

4

Lungs Expand

Visceral pleura pulls with parietal pleura due to surface tension. Alveolar volume increases.

5

Pressure Decreases

Intrapulmonary pressure drops to ~758 mmHg (2 mmHg below atmospheric).

6

Air Flows In

Air moves from higher atmospheric pressure to lower alveolar pressure until pressures equalize.

Deep Inspiration Accessory Muscles:

  • Sternocleidomastoid: Elevates sternum
  • Scalenes: Elevate first two ribs
  • Pectoralis Minor: Elevates ribs 3-5

Expiration (Exhalation)

Quiet Expiration (Passive):

1

Muscles Relax

Diaphragm and external intercostals relax passively.

2

Elastic Recoil

Lungs and chest wall return to resting position due to elastic fibers in lung tissue and surface tension in alveoli.

3

Thoracic Volume Decreases

Chest cavity becomes smaller, intrapleural pressure becomes less negative (~-4 mmHg).

4

Pressure Increases

Intrapulmonary pressure rises to ~762 mmHg (2 mmHg above atmospheric).

5

Air Flows Out

Air moves from higher alveolar pressure to lower atmospheric pressure.

Forced Expiration (Active):

  • Internal Intercostals: Depress rib cage
  • Abdominal Muscles: Contract to push diaphragm upward
    • Rectus abdominis
    • External obliques
    • Internal obliques
    • Transversus abdominis

Lung Volumes and Capacities

Measured using spirometry, these values vary with age, sex, height, and fitness level.

Dead Space:

  • Anatomical Dead Space: Air in conducting airways that doesn't participate in gas exchange (~150 mL)
  • Alveolar Dead Space: Air in non-functional alveoli (normally minimal)
  • Physiological Dead Space: Anatomical + Alveolar dead space

Clinical Significance

Alveolar ventilation is more important than total ventilation for gas exchange. Rapid, shallow breathing moves air mainly in dead space, while slow, deep breathing maximizes alveolar ventilation.

Factors Affecting Pulmonary Ventilation

1. Airway Resistance:

  • Bronchiole Diameter: Primary determinant
    • Sympathetic stimulation ‚Üí bronchodilation (Œ≤‚ÇÇ receptors)
    • Parasympathetic stimulation ‚Üí bronchoconstriction (muscarinic receptors)
    • Histamine ‚Üí bronchoconstriction (allergic reactions)
  • Mucus and Debris: Increases resistance
  • Lung Volume: Higher volume = wider airways = less resistance

2. Lung Compliance:

Measure of lung distensibility - how easily lungs expand.

  • High Compliance: Lungs expand easily (e.g., emphysema)
  • Low Compliance: Lungs resist expansion (e.g., fibrosis)
  • Factors Decreasing Compliance:
    • Decreased surfactant production
    • Pulmonary fibrosis (scarring)
    • Pulmonary edema (fluid in lungs)
    • Decreased chest wall elasticity

3. Alveolar Surface Tension:

  • Without Surfactant: Surface tension would collapse small alveoli
  • Law of LaPlace: Smaller alveoli have higher pressure (P = 2T/r)
  • Surfactant Effect: Reduces surface tension more in smaller alveoli, preventing collapse and equalizing pressures

Regulation of Respiration

Respiratory Centers in the Brain:

  • Medullary Rhythmicity Centers (Medulla Oblongata):
    • Dorsal Respiratory Group (DRG): Controls inspiration; sets basic rhythm
    • Ventral Respiratory Group (VRG): Inactive during quiet breathing; active during forced breathing (controls both inspiration and expiration)
  • Pontine Respiratory Centers (Pons):
    • Pneumotaxic Center: Inhibits inspiration, fine-tunes rhythm
    • Apneustic Center: Stimulates inspiration, promotes deep breathing

Common Respiratory Conditions

Obstructive Lung Diseases

Characterized by airflow limitation, usually due to airway narrowing or loss of elastic recoil.

Asthma

Cause: Chronic airway inflammation with reversible bronchoconstriction

Triggers: Allergens, exercise, cold air, stress

Symptoms: Wheezing, dyspnea, chest tightness, cough

Treatment: Bronchodilators (Œ≤‚ÇÇ agonists), corticosteroids

Chronic Bronchitis

Cause: Chronic inflammation of bronchi, excessive mucus production

Definition: Productive cough for ‚â•3 months in 2 consecutive years

Symptoms: Chronic cough with sputum, dyspnea, cyanosis ("blue bloaters")

Risk Factor: Smoking (90% of cases)

Emphysema

Cause: Destruction of alveolar walls, loss of elastic recoil

Mechanism: Protease/antiprotease imbalance

Symptoms: Dyspnea, barrel chest, pursed-lip breathing ("pink puffers")

Finding: Reduced surface area for gas exchange

COPD

Definition: Combination of chronic bronchitis and emphysema

Characteristic: Progressive, irreversible airflow limitation

Spirometry: Reduced FEV‚ÇÅ/FVC ratio (< 70%)

Management: Smoking cessation, bronchodilators, O‚ÇÇ therapy

Restrictive Lung Diseases

Characterized by reduced lung expansion and decreased total lung capacity.

Pulmonary Fibrosis

Cause: Scarring of lung tissue, thickening of alveolar walls

Result: Reduced compliance, impaired gas exchange

Causes: Idiopathic, occupational exposures, medications

Symptoms: Progressive dyspnea, dry cough, clubbing

Pneumoconiosis

Types: Asbestosis, silicosis, coal worker's pneumoconiosis

Cause: Inhalation of mineral dusts

Mechanism: Inflammation ‚Üí fibrosis

Prevention: Occupational safety measures

Sarcoidosis

Cause: Inflammatory disease with granuloma formation

Affected: Lungs, lymph nodes, other organs

Symptoms: Variable; may be asymptomatic or severe

Diagnosis: Chest X-ray, biopsy

Respiratory Distress Syndrome

Neonatal RDS: Surfactant deficiency in premature infants

Adult ARDS: Acute lung injury, diffuse alveolar damage

Treatment: Mechanical ventilation, surfactant therapy (neonatal)

Mortality: High in ARDS (30-40%)

Infectious Diseases

Pneumonia

Cause: Infection of alveoli (bacterial, viral, fungal)

Common Bacteria: Streptococcus pneumoniae, Haemophilus influenzae

Symptoms: Fever, cough with sputum, dyspnea, chest pain

Finding: Consolidation on chest X-ray

Tuberculosis (TB)

Cause: Mycobacterium tuberculosis

Transmission: Airborne droplets

Symptoms: Chronic cough, hemoptysis, night sweats, weight loss

Treatment: Multi-drug antibiotic therapy (6-9 months)

Influenza

Cause: Influenza virus (types A, B, C)

Symptoms: Fever, myalgia, cough, sore throat

Complications: Secondary bacterial pneumonia

Prevention: Annual vaccination

COVID-19

Cause: SARS-CoV-2 virus

Symptoms: Variable; fever, cough, dyspnea, loss of taste/smell

Severe: ARDS, multi-organ failure

Prevention: Vaccination, hygiene measures

Other Conditions

Lung Cancer

Types: Small cell lung carcinoma (SCLC), non-small cell lung carcinoma (NSCLC)

Risk Factors: Smoking (85% of cases), radon, asbestos, air pollution

Symptoms: Often late; cough, hemoptysis, dyspnea, weight loss

Prognosis: Often poor; 5-year survival ~20%

Prevention: Smoking cessation most important

Pulmonary Embolism (PE)

Cause: Blood clot blocking pulmonary artery (often from deep vein thrombosis)

Symptoms: Sudden dyspnea, chest pain, tachycardia, hypoxemia

Diagnosis: CT pulmonary angiography, D-dimer

Treatment: Anticoagulation, thrombolysis (severe cases)

Risk Factors: Immobility, surgery, pregnancy, clotting disorders

Pleural Effusion

Definition: Excess fluid in pleural cavity

Types: Transudative (heart failure, cirrhosis) vs. Exudative (infection, cancer)

Symptoms: Dyspnea, pleuritic chest pain, cough

Diagnosis: Chest X-ray, thoracentesis (fluid analysis)

Treatment: Treat underlying cause, drainage if large

Pneumothorax

Definition: Air in pleural cavity causing lung collapse

Types:

  • Spontaneous: Rupture of bleb (young, tall, thin males)
  • Traumatic: Penetrating chest injury, rib fracture
  • Tension: Life-threatening; progressive air accumulation with mediastinal shift

Symptoms: Sudden chest pain, dyspnea, decreased breath sounds

Treatment: Chest tube insertion, needle decompression (tension)

Sleep Apnea

Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA): Upper airway collapse during sleep

Central Sleep Apnea: Brain fails to signal breathing muscles

Symptoms: Loud snoring, daytime sleepiness, morning headaches

Complications: Hypertension, heart disease, stroke

Treatment: CPAP (continuous positive airway pressure), weight loss
